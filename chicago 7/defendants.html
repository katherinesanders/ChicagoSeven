The Defendants

David Dellinger

David Dellinger was the oldest of the Chicago 7. In court, he seemed more sophisticated than the rest, wearing a suit and tie. He has an impressive education, attending Yale and spending one year at Oxford on a scholarship, then returning to Yale for a graduate degree later in life. During his time at the Ivy, Dellinger would frequently serve time in jail for his refusal to sign up for the draft. Dellinger was an activist in all areas, supporting the right to unionize in the 1930s and protesting for civil rights. He was an organizer of the National Committee to End the War in Vietnam. Dellinger had negotiated three American prisoners out of North Vietnam, and escorted them back to the United States with Tom Hayden and Rennie Davis. He was seen as an energetic and genuinely happy person by the people who met him.


Rennie Davis

Rennie Davis was born in Michigan, and graduated from Oberlin College. He was a member of the Students for a Democratic Society and the National Mobilization Committee to End the War in Vietnam. He attended many rallies and protests, and organized many as well. With Tom Hayden, Davis went to a conference in Czechoslovakia and both Hayden and Davis along with David Dellinger were invited by North Vietnamese leadership to escort American prisoners back to the United States. In his later life, Davis became a promoter of Guru Maharaj Ji, a speaker who claimed to have a great power of meditation.


Tom Hayden

Tom Hayden was the founder of the Students for a Democratic Society, and his passion for the anti-war movement started during his time at the University of Michigan after witnessing the witch hunts of the Human Un-American Activities Committee. Hayden was also a civil rights activist participating in sit-ins, voter registration drives, and the Freedom Riders. Along with Davis and Dellinger, Hayden attended a conference in Czechoslovakia and escorted American prisoners out of North Vietnam. He became a politician later in life, winning a seat in the California senate. He wrote over 20 books and taught at California Universities and Harvard.


Jerry Rubin

Jerry Rubin grew up as a very talented writer and became a reporter for the Cincinnati Star to support his younger brother when his parents passed away. His far left political opinion started when his aunt took him to Cuba where he met Che Guevara, the Minister of Industries and a notable marxist. After this, he moved to Berkeley. Rubin organized Berkeley’s Vietnam Day Committee and held the world’s largest teach-in against the Vietnam War. With Abbie Hoffman, Rubin founded the Youth International Party, or YIPPIES. Rubin and Hoffman’s “festival of life” in Chicago is what ended them up in court. Rubin passed away in his 50s after getting hit by a car in Los Angeles.


Abbie Hoffman

Abbie Hoffman was most likely considered the most unruly of the Chicago 7. He often interrupted and mocked Judge Hoffman, and was not serious when testifying. Earlier in his life, he worked for the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee. However, he decided to live a more flamboyant life. Hoffman was the founder of the Youth International Party, or YIPPIES. He worked closely with Jerry Rubin in this group. One elaborate stunt was when Hoffman and Rubin and many others claimed they would “levitate the pentagon.” Hoffman was heavily involved with LSD. After the trial, he published a book titled “Steal This Book.” He died due to suicide after struggling with Bipolar Disorder.


Lee Weiner

Lee Weiner was one of the forgettable members of the Chicago 7, but the only one from Chicago. Weiner was not part of an organization, but did protest in Chicago that day. He faced charges for his attempt to teach protestors how to use potentially dangerous weapons. In an interview, Weiner explained that his closest friends were Hoffman and Rubin, and they often had troubles with Hayden. Weiner had troubles with Hayden later on in life too, often criticizing his political decisions.

John Froines

John Froines was considered a forgettable member of the Chicago 7 as he was not the organizer of any larger organization. However, he was a member of the Students for a Democratic Society. Froines was a chemist, receiving a masters from UC Berkeley and a PhD from Yale. In his later life, he worked as a Professor at UCLA.
